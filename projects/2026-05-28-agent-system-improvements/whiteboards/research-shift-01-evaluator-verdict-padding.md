# Whiteboard — shift 01: evaluator verdict padding

**Topic.** Judgment-heavy `/ev-loop-interactive` units pay an attention tax during `/guild-validate` because each evaluator in the panel exhaustively cycles through its full rubric in verdict prose even when only 2-3 dimensions are load-bearing for that unit. The operator reads through irrelevant "no findings in [dimension]" sections looking for the load-bearing findings.

**Substrate evidence.**
- `plugins/ev/skills/ev-loop-interactive/SKILL.md:295-349` — three-section dense packet (efficiency / contract / signals); no rubric-scope section.
- `plugins/guild/agents/personalities/personality-base.md` — no scope/applicability framing; verdicts unconditionally cover the full rubric.
- `plugins/guild/agents/retained/evaluator-contract-fit.md` — `scope` only appears as a flag classification (`scope-creep`), never as a panel-time narrowing mechanism.
- `plugins/guild/docs/PANEL-COMPOSITION.md:284-299` — per-phase and per-unit `Panel:` overrides ALREADY EXIST as an escape hatch.

## whiteboard-substrate-engineer

The three fix shapes land in three different layers of the substrate, and the layer choice matters more than which one is "right" — each one trades blast radius for leverage differently.

**Fix 1 (Rubric scope section in the dense packet)** lives in the orchestrator: it's a packet-shape change in `plugins/ev/skills/ev-loop-interactive/SKILL.md:301-344`. The packet is the contract between `/ev-loop-interactive` and `/guild-validate` — a new `## Rubric scope` section is additive, soft-extension shaped, and doesn't touch any evaluator agent body. Append-only invariants are untouched (packets are session-ephemeral, not substrate state). The blast radius is tiny — one skill file, one PR — but the leverage is also bounded: evaluators have to be taught to honor the section, and nothing in `personality-base.md` or `evaluator-base.md` currently tells them to read scope-narrowing instructions from the packet. Without a corresponding read-side change, the section is decorative.

**Fix 2 (evaluators derive scope from the Contract section)** lives in the artifact: it's a body change in every `evaluator-*.md` (or, more substrate-respectfully, in `evaluator-base.md` since that's where the shared verdict shape and stance live — and `guild/agents/personalities/personality-base.md` is where cross-cutting evaluator framing is supposed to compose in via codegen). This is the highest-leverage fix because it changes behavior across the whole family with one edit to the shared base, and it leans on the dense packet's existing `## Contract (paraphrased)` section rather than inventing a new section. But it has the highest blast radius shape-wise: it changes the verdict contract for every evaluator simultaneously, which is exactly the kind of family-wide change that needs a schema bump or at least a coordinated cutover. Verdicts are ephemeral panel outputs — migration risk is "do all evaluators behave consistently after the change," not "do old verdicts still parse." Manageable, but not free.

**Fix 3 (per-tier presets including a new `evaluator-substrate-engineer`)** is two fixes wearing one hat, and they want to be split. The "per-tier preset" half is already substrate-supported: `docs/PANEL-COMPOSITION.md:284-299` documents both per-phase `panel:` override in `PLAN.md` and per-unit `Panel:` override in the contract. Mechanical units don't get a stripped panel today not because the substrate can't express it, but because no one's reaching for the affordance. That's the lowest-blast-radius nudge in this whole brief: write a `docs/` note or a learning that says "for mechanical units, declare `panel: [evaluator-contract-fit]` and skip the rest." Zero code. The "new `evaluator-substrate-engineer`" half is a different conversation entirely — a new family member, with all the family-shape consistency questions (does it follow `evaluator-*` naming, does it compose from the same personality/phase/domain axes as the others, what's its rubric source-of-truth), and the meta-irony that the substrate-engineer role prompt explicitly says "there is no `evaluator-substrate-engineer`" because substrate concerns are design-phase, not review-phase. If that statement is still load-bearing, this third sub-fix shouldn't exist; if it's been outgrown, the fix is "introduce the family member" not "add it as a panel preset," and that's its own brief.

**Lowest blast radius:** lean into the existing `Panel:` override affordance — it's already in the substrate, costs a doc nudge to surface, and any unit can opt into a narrower panel today. **Highest leverage:** teach the evaluator base (`evaluator-base.md` if it surfaces, otherwise `personality-base.md`'s evaluator phase section) to derive scope from the Contract section of the packet and emit verdict prose only for in-scope dimensions. Fix 1's `## Rubric scope` section is real but probably redundant once Fix 2 lands — the contract's acceptance criteria already are the scope statement, and adding a parallel section invites the two to drift.

**Skeptical note on the framing.** "Attention tax" is the operator's complaint, but the evaluator-base reason for exhaustive verdict prose is probably that *the evaluator doesn't know what's load-bearing for this unit*. Pre-computed verification in the packet is authoritative; the verdict's job is partly to say "I checked dimension X and found nothing" so the operator can trust the absence. Strip the no-findings sections and the verdict becomes silently underspecified — was that dimension out of scope, or was it in scope and clean? Without an explicit scope declaration that the evaluator echoes back ("scope: [acceptance criteria 2, 3]; not evaluated: rubric dimensions a, b, c"), the operator loses a confidence signal they currently get for free. The cleanest version of Fix 2 makes the evaluator's scope declaration *more* visible, not less — short, structured, scannable, near the top of the verdict — rather than just removing the no-findings sections wholesale. That preserves the trust signal while killing the prose padding.

## whiteboard-skeptic

Three pressure points worth holding in tension before the panel converges on "rubric-narrowing at invocation":

**The padding might be the feature.** An evaluator that exhaustively cycles its rubric catches the dimension the operator didn't know to scope in. That's the entire point of an antagonist panel — it surfaces concerns *the unit contract didn't anticipate*. If we narrow at panel invocation, we lose the "wait, you also broke X" failure mode, which is the one a careful human reviewer would catch and the one CI can't see. The operator paying attention tax on a verdict that turns out to be in-scope is annoying; the operator merging a unit because the narrowed panel didn't look at the dimension that actually regressed is a meaningfully worse outcome. Name that failure mode explicitly in the design before optimizing it away — "surprise concern surfaced by out-of-scope dimension" is the load-bearing behavior we'd be trading off.

**The diagnosis might be misplaced one layer up.** If contracts arrived at `/guild-validate` with sharp acceptance criteria ("this unit changes the contract-fit surface; a11y and tokens are not in scope"), the panel would have the information to self-narrow without a new rubric-scope mechanism. The fact that operators feel the need to narrow at invocation time suggests contracts are arriving *under-specified* and the panel is dutifully filling the gap by cycling everything. The missing feature might be contract-quality scaffolding — a `/ev-loop-interactive` step that pressure-tests the contract's scope declaration before spawning evaluators — not a rubric-narrowing knob on the evaluators themselves. That fix compounds better: every downstream consumer of the contract benefits, not just the panel.

**Lock-in risk on the narrowing API.** If we add a `rubric-scope` field to the dense packet at `SKILL.md:295-349`, evaluators will start branching on it, operators will learn to set it aggressively to dodge attention tax, and the "exhaustive panel" default quietly becomes the "narrow panel" default because nobody opts back in. Six months from now the question "does our panel actually evaluate a11y on this unit?" has a non-obvious answer depending on how the contract was drafted. If we go this route, the design needs an obvious naming hook and an invariant comment — something like "narrowing is a hint, not a gate; evaluators still flag out-of-scope concerns as advisory" — or the feature drifts from "save attention" to "skip evaluators."

The cleanest move is probably to try the upstream fix first (contract-quality scaffolding) and only reach for evaluator-layer narrowing if contracts arrive sharp and operators *still* report the tax. If we skip straight to rubric-scope, we're optimizing the symptom and locking in an API that's hard to walk back.
